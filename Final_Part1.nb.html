For this portion of the final exam, I will be conducting principal component analysis (PCA) on the climate csv file. Principal component analysis is a statistical technique that allows you to understand which components of a dataset account for the most variance. It is a technique that is often used to understand datasets that have a lot of variables because it allows you to transform or simplify lots of variables into composite components.

By using PCA, I can explore the following questions about the climate dataset:

  1. Which components and variables account for most of the data’s variability?
  2. Which and how many variables could we omit in the dataset while preserving as much information as possible?

To answer the questions above, we need to understand how much variance different components account for. The greater the variance, the more information the data is explaining.

First, I prepare the R workspace for this analysis. Set the working directory, load in the data and required libraries, and clean up the data to remove any columns which are unnecessary for our analysis. I removed the FIA plot and location columns.

And by looking at the values for each variable for PC1, the elevation (meters) variable accounts for nearly all of PC1, which means it is the most associated with PC1. Then, by including the second component, nearly 100% of the variability is explained. And by looking at the values for each variable for PC2, the mean annual precipitation (cm) variable accounts for almost all of PC2, which means it is th most associated with PC2.

The scree plot is a helpful visualization tool for deciding how many principal components we need to include. Where the line levels off in the plot, we can choose to include the principal components, but it does not add much to the analysis. In the case of this data, the line begins to level off around the 3rd or 4th principal component.

The biplot is another visualization tool to help us understand which variables are the most associated with principal components. Specifically, the biplot tells us which variables are most associated with PC1 and PC2. To understand which variables are most associated, the variables that are separated across the X axis are the most associated with PC1 and the variables separated vertically along the ^ axis are the most associated with PC2.

Elevation (meters) is separated across the X axis, which means it is the most associated with PC1, and mean annual precipitation is separated across the Y axis, which means it is the most associated with PC2. Another important aspect that this plot demonstrates is the strength of the elevation variable which tells us that this variable accounts for a lot of variability.

All of this information matches our current understanding of the variables which we also gleaned from the summary statement of the PCA.

At the beginning of this exercise, I mentioned that there were two main questions I wanted to answer: 1). Which components and variables account for most of the data’s variability? 2). Which and how many variables could we omit in the data set while preserving as much information as possible?

From our analysis, we know that just the first two components account for ane explain nearly all of the variance in the dataset. Furthermore, we know that elevation (m) explains nearly all of PC1 and the average annual precipitation (cm) explains nearly all of PC2. Therefore, these variables are the most important predictors of this data, and omitting the rest of the principal components and variables would not remove much information in the dataset.
